## Version B (Full — 300 words)

Every Air Force base in the world runs on infrastructure. Runways, taxiways, hangars, utilities, housing, fuel distribution systems, hardened aircraft shelters. Someone has to design, build, maintain, and repair all of it, often on compressed timelines and in contested environments.

That's the work of Air Force civil engineers. And today, we celebrate 79 years of it. ✈️

Happy 79th Birthday to the U.S. Air Force, established September 18, 1947.

When the Air Force became its own branch, it inherited a critical truth from the Army Air Corps: air power depends on infrastructure. Without runways, there are no sorties. Without fuel systems, aircraft don't fly. Without housing and utilities, Airmen can't sustain operations. Civil engineers make all of it possible.

Two units stand out in that mission. RED HORSE (Rapid Engineer Deployable Heavy Operational Repair Squadron Engineers) deploys to build and repair airfield infrastructure in expeditionary environments. These are heavy construction units that can stand up a base from bare ground. Prime BEEF (Prime Base Engineer Emergency Force) squadrons handle base engineering, emergency management, and facility sustainment at installations worldwide.

Together, they represent a civil engineering capability that rivals anything in the private sector, operating under conditions most civilian engineers will never face.

At SAME San Francisco Post, our mission is to connect military engineers with the A/E/C profession. Air Force civil engineers bring deep experience in rapid construction, infrastructure resilience, and large-scale project delivery. When they transition to the private sector, they make our industry stronger. When they're still in uniform, our partnerships help them accomplish the mission.

To every Airman who has worn the civil engineering beret: happy 79th. Your work keeps the mission flying.
